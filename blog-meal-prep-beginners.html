Meal Prep for Beginners: The 90-Minute Sunday System

Search "meal prep" and you'll find people cooking fourteen identical chicken-and-rice boxes on a Sunday afternoon. That works — for about three weeks, until the sameness breaks you. After years of reader feedback, we're convinced the sustainable version is different: don't prep meals, prep components. Ninety minutes on Sunday, and every weeknight dinner becomes assembly instead of cooking.

Why component prep beats meal prep

A finished meal locks you in: Tuesday-you must want exactly what Sunday-you decided. Components stay flexible. Roasted vegetables can top a grain bowl Monday, fill tacos Wednesday, and land in a frittata Friday. Same fridge, three different dinners — and nothing feels like leftovers.

The 90-minute Sunday system

The trick is running four tracks at once. Here's the sequence:

  1. Minutes 0–10 — Start the grain. One big pot of rice, farro or quinoa (2–3 cups dry). While it simmers, everything else happens.
  2. Minutes 10–30 — Load the oven. Two sheet pans of vegetables (whatever's in season, cut chunky, olive oil, salt) at 425°F. Set a timer for 25 minutes and forget them.
  3. Minutes 30–60 — Cook one protein. A tray of chicken thighs, a pot of lentils, or a dozen hard-boiled eggs — pick one, not three. Ambition is the enemy of week two.
  4. Minutes 60–80 — Make one sauce and one pickle. A jar of vinaigrette or yogurt-herb sauce, plus quick-pickled onions (sliced onion, warm vinegar, pinch of sugar and salt). These two jars are what make components taste like meals.
  5. Minutes 80–90 — Wash greens, pack everything. Cool components before lidding so nothing steams itself soggy.

What NOT to prep

  • Dressed salads: they're compost by Tuesday. Prep the parts, dress at dinner.
  • Cut avocado, sliced apples: brown and sad within hours. Cut fresh, it takes 40 seconds.
  • Pasta: cooked pasta ages badly. Cook it the night you need it — it's 10 minutes — and use your prepped sauce and vegetables.
  • Anything fried or crispy: refrigeration is where crispness goes to die.

The container reality check

You don't need the 36-piece set. Six glass containers with real seals, two large jars for sauces and pickles, and one big greens box. Glass matters less for health than for morale — you can see what you have, so you actually use it.

Your first week's menu, assembled

From that one Sunday session: grain bowls with roasted vegetables and yogurt sauce (Monday), protein plus greens salad with vinaigrette (Tuesday), tacos with pickled onions (Wednesday), fried rice from the grain and odds and ends (Thursday), and a frittata that gracefully retires whatever's left (Friday).

Ninety minutes. Five dinners mostly done. And because nothing was locked into a box, none of it felt repeated. That's the system — boring on Sunday so your weeknights don't have to be.
