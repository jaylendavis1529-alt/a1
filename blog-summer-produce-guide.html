The Summer Produce Guide: What to Buy in July & August

If the produce year has a crescendo, it's right now. July and August are when tomatoes actually taste like tomatoes and peaches drip down your wrist. But peak season is also peak perishability — the same fruit that took months to ripen can be gone in two days on your counter. Here's the field guide we use ourselves.

Tomatoes: the main event

How to pick: smell the stem end — a real tomato smells green and sweet there. Heavy for its size beats big. Cosmetic cracks are fine; soft spots aren't.

How to store: never in the fridge — cold kills the enzymes responsible for flavor. Counter, stem-side down, out of direct sun.

Fastest great dish: thick slices, flaky salt, good olive oil, torn basil. That's it — don't cook away the thing you waited ten months for.

Stone fruit: peaches, plums, nectarines

How to pick: gentle give at the shoulder, honeyed smell. Skip anything with a green tinge near the stem — it was picked too soon and will go straight from hard to mealy.

How to store: ripen on the counter in a single layer; refrigerate only once fully ripe, and eat within two days.

Fastest great dish: halve, grill cut-side down for 3 minutes, serve with yogurt and honey. Dessert that thinks it's health food.

Corn: buy it, cook it, same day

Corn's sugars start converting to starch the moment it's picked, so the farmers' market beats the supermarket by a mile here. Look for tight green husks and sticky, pale silk. Don't peel the husk back in the store — you're just drying out someone's dinner. Boil 3 minutes, or grill in the husk for 12. If you're feeling ambitious, cut it raw into salads — peak-season corn needs no cooking at all.

Zucchini: the overachiever

Small and medium zucchini beat the baseball bats every time — big ones are watery and bitter-seeded. Store in the fridge crisper in a paper bag. When the inevitable glut hits, our two favorite escapes: coins seared hard in a dry pan until charred (then dressed with lemon and parmesan), or grated raw into fritters. And yes, zucchini shrinks by half when cooked — buy more than looks reasonable.

The don't-forget list

  • Cucumbers: smash-and-salt them with rice vinegar for the summer's easiest side.
  • Green beans: blanch, shock, then blister in a hot pan with garlic.
  • Melons: a ripe cantaloupe smells like cantaloupe at the blossom end. No smell, no buy.
  • Fresh herbs: basil hates the fridge (counter, in water, like flowers); everything else keeps wrapped in a damp towel.

Shop the glut, cook the glut

Whatever's cheapest and piled highest is what's at peak. Build the week around that pile — it's the single best flavor-per-dollar decision you can make, and it's exactly how our weekday recipes get planned each summer.
